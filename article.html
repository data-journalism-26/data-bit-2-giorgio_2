How the word "Remigration" entered the Italian parliament

For two years, the word remigration (Italian: remigrazione) remained a fringe idea and sat outside plenary debate, including in Italy. Then, the opposition started using it to accuse political opponents, until, on 1 April 2026, a Fratelli d'Italia (FdI, Brothers of Italy) senator reasserted the idea during a public session.

Giorgio Coppola 6 May 2026

On 1 April 2026 senator Marco Lisei (Fratelli d'Italia) rose in the Senate to thank the European Parliament. On 26 March, the European Parliament voted the Italian line on remigration, on repatriation, and on third-country structures, he said. He framed Italy as the architect of an emerging European deportation regime — and embraced a word that until that morning had belonged to neonazi street groups.

Remigration is the polite name for forcibly deporting people — including legal residents and citizens — on ethnic grounds. But the word is not new: the French writer Renaud Camus coined it alongside the idea of the «grand remplacement" — or “ethnic replacement” — has long since ceased to be a taboo idea, the sort that could never be mentioned on a television talk show or, even less so, in the halls of any institution, starting with Parliament. The "Great Replacement" is a conspiracy theory that claims that elites are deliberately swapping native populations for non-white migrants. French Identitarians carried it into mainstream politics through the 2010s; Austrian activist Martin Sellner gave it a three-phase manifesto, Remigration: A Proposal; in 2022 French presidential candidate Éric Zemmour proposed a Ministry of Remigration. By 2025 the Austrian FPÖ, Germany's AfD and other European far-right parties used it openly; in the US, the Trump administration's Department of Homeland Security used it in official communications.

On 15 January 2024, a jury of linguists at the Philipps-Universität Marburg named Remigration the German Unwort des Jahres (Non-Word of the Year) for 2023, with the following justification: The term Remigration is deliberately co-opted ideologically and reinterpreted in such a way as to disguise a — politically demanded — inhumane practice of expulsion and deportation. The same word was at the centre of Correctiv's Potsdam scoop on the AfD a few weeks later.

However, according to a January 2026 report by the Center on Strategic Online Hate, social-media mentions of the term jumped roughly forty-fold — from about 32,000 across the six years before 2023 to 1.4 million in the three years after, peaking at 71,000 in a single week last September. The trajectory the report describes — a fringe slogan crossing into mainstream office through repetition, institutional validation, and algorithmic amplification — is what Italy is now joining late. Here we show how.

A scrape of every Camera stenografico plus every Senato resoconto d'aula returns 68 paragraphs containing the term, all between April 2024 and May 2026. The full XVIII Legislatura — the four years when Matteo Salvini's anti-migrant rhetoric dominated — yields zero. The first Senate trace is a written question, filed in April 2024 by senators Unterberger and Spagnolli about Martin Sellner's planned Italian visits. The first plenary utterance comes nine months later, on 8 January 2025, when Lega deputy Rossano Sasso took the floor to tie a call for forced removal to alleged sexual harassment of girls reported on the night of 31 December 2024 — incidents covered by Italian outlets and most prominently the Piazza Duomo case in Milan, where prosecutors opened an investigation into the assault of a small group of foreign tourists. Sasso said: The time has come to act even more harshly and to react. […] We must tell those who hate Italy, those who do not accept our laws, those who molest young girls on New Year's Eve, that they are no longer welcome. We must increase the number of repatriations, support the police, and tell certain criminals that for them the only solution is called remigration.

The two sides use the word differently. Opposition benches (PD, M5S, AVS, Azione, +Europa) — 48 of 68 mentions, over 70% — use it as an indictment: a charge against a government they say has already enacted what the word means. The governing coalition's seven utterances — Sasso × 5, Furgiuele defending the cancelled Remigrazione e Riconquista conference, and Lisei — do the opposite: they appropriate the word, claiming it as a legitimate name for the policy.

Remigration is one word in a longer Italian vocabulary of migration framing. Older terms — invasione (invasion), immigrazione clandestina (illegal immigration), sostituzione etnica (the Italian rendering of the ethnic replacement idea introduced above) — have been part of Italian political discourse for years, with their own, longer arcs. A side-by-side history of those terms would be a different piece. What is distinct here is that remigration has no pre-2024 baseline to recover — the chart above is watching an entry from zero rather than the latest leg of an older curve.

Lisei's speech crosses the line CasaPound was kept from crossing. He spoke the word, claimed it for the governing party, and the chamber voted on. The U.S. parallel is already operational. The Trump administration's Department of Homeland Security is, on the Center on Strategic Online Hate's reading, the first national government to use the word remigration in formal communications — and its enforcement arm, ICE, is enacting the policy at scale.

Data and methods

This piece relies on two primary sources — the stenographic records of the Camera dei Deputati and the Akoma Ntoso bulk export of the Senato della Repubblica — passed through a small R + shell pipeline before the timeline figure renders the curated moments. The walkthrough below is in build order.

1. Sources

  • Camera dei Deputati — plenary resoconti stenografici from the official portal www.camera.it, one HTML page per session at documenti.camera.it/leg{LL}/resoconti/assemblea/html/sed{NNNN}/stenografico.htm.
  • Senato della Repubblica — official Akoma Ntoso bulk repository SenatoDellaRepubblica/AkomaNtosoBulkData, one resaula/*-ra.akn.xml file per resoconto d'aula.
  • Camera deputy schede — biographical and group-history pages at documenti.camera.it/apps/commonServices/getDocumento.ashx?sezione=deputati&tipoDoc=schedaDeputato&idLegislatura=19&idPersona={id}, used to attribute each speech to the speaker's parliamentary group at the date of the speech.

2. Search pattern

A single case-insensitive regex — remigra[a-z]* — is applied to every paragraph. It matches all morphological forms (remigrazione, remigrare, remigrato, remigrazioni, …) without false positives in Italian. The pattern is defined once in code/00-setup.R and reused by every downstream step.

3. Camera scrape

Camera publishes no bulk export, public API, or machine-readable index of plenary URLs. By inspecting session pages opened from www.camera.it, the URL is observed to follow a fixed template:

https://documenti.camera.it/leg{LL}/resoconti/assemblea/html/sed{NNNN}/stenografico.htm

where {LL} is the legislature (XVIII or XIX in scope) and {NNNN} the zero-padded session number. The shell pipeline code/01-scrape-camera.sh (curl + xargs, twelve parallel workers) probes every pair — XVIII 1–750, XIX 1–700 — discards 404s and sub-5 KB stubs, stream-greps each remaining body for case-insensitive remigra, and writes only matches to data/raw/camera/. Re-runs are idempotent; the XIX ceiling is bumped by hand as new sessions accumulate.

Tooling disclosure. Claude Code (Anthropic) was used to identify the URL template and scaffold the probe-and-filter script; URLs were spot-checked against the live portal before the full scrape ran.

4. Camera extraction

For each retained Camera HTML hit (code/02-extract-camera.R), the parser walks <p class="intervento"> and <p class="interventoVirtuale"> elements and attributes every matching paragraph to its speaker. The speaker block carries an idPersona identifier, which is used downstream to look up the deputy's group history. Output: data/processed/camera_hits.csv, 54 paragraph-level rows.

5. Senato extraction

The Senato bulk repo is sparse-checked-out for legislatures XVIII and XIX (code/03-extract-senato.R). The parser walks each Akoma Ntoso XML document and applies two attribution rules:

  • Matches inside <an:speech> are attributed to the speaker named in the corresponding <an:from> (and to the (GROUP) tag preceding the first speech paragraph).
  • Matches in Allegato B (written questions and answers) are tagged allegato_b: the source XML carries no paragraph-level signer for these, so they enter the dataset unattributed at the speaker level.

Output: data/processed/senato_hits.csv, 14 rows (3 plenary speeches + 11 Allegato B paragraphs).

6. Party attribution at the time of the speech

For each Camera deputy (code/04-party-lookup.R) the script parses the scheda for the full group history with start/end dates, then selects the group active on the speech date. This matters when a deputy switches group mid-legislature — e.g. Rossano Sasso, who left Lega for Misto on 6 February 2026: paragraphs before that date are tagged Lega, after Misto. Output: data/processed/all_hits.csv, the joined Camera + Senato dataset (68 rows) with party and government/opposition coalition flags.

7. Curation: eight moments from sixty-eight

The full machine-readable dataset is 68 paragraphs across 17 plenary sessions. The figure shows eight, picked for the trajectory they trace rather than for raw frequency: each one marks a first, a breakthrough, or the peak.

  • 30 Apr 2024 — first time the word enters any Senato document, in a written question on Martin Sellner.
  • 8 Jan 2025 — first Camera plenary utterance, by Lega deputy Sasso.
  • 4 Jun 2025 — first Senate plenary use, by AVS senator De Cristofaro, as attack.
  • 5 Nov 2025 — AVS deputy Grimaldi reports a right-wing colleague using the term inside the Aula.
  • 30 Jan 2026 — PD deputy Casu speaks on the day of the cancelled CasaPound conference.
  • 3 Feb 2026 — Lega deputy Furgiuele defends from the floor the conference he had hosted.
  • 1 Apr 2026 — first Fratelli d'Italia senator (Lisei) to embrace the term approvingly in plenary. The breakthrough.
  • 22 Apr 2026 — peak session of the dataset (29 paragraphs); Sasso, now Misto, scolds his old coalition.

8. Figure

The visualisation is a vertical quote timeline rather than a chart. Each dot is one moment the word entered the parliamentary record; vertical position is chronology, dot colour is party, and — crucially — the content of each card is the speech itself. A strip plot was tried and discarded: it could show when and who, but not what, and the what is the whole point.

9. Caveats

  • Scope: plenary records only (committee transcripts out of scope; spot-checking Senato sommcomm returns zero matches). Earlier legislatures (XVII and before) excluded — the term entered European far-right vocabulary only around 2014 and is absent from Italian parliamentary discourse before April 2024.
  • Allegato B paragraphs are not signed at the paragraph level in the Senato Akoma Ntoso source. Those hits are tagged allegato_b and labelled accordingly in the figure, rather than attributed to a guessed speaker.
  • Speakers who switched parliamentary group mid-legislature are attributed to the group active at the date of the speech.

10. Reproducibility

All code and data at github.com/data-journalism-26/data-bit-2-giorgio_2. Run make (incremental, only rebuilds stale steps) or Rscript code/run_all.R (single-shot) — both are idempotent and re-use cached HTML, the Senato sparse clone, and the deputy schede.
