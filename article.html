The non-word that finally entered the Italian parliament

For two years remigrazione sat outside Italian plenary debate — except as an insult thrown at the right. Then, on 1 April 2026, an FdI senator embraced it.

Giorgio Coppola 6 May 2026

On 1 April 2026 senator Marco Lisei (Fratelli d'Italia) rose in the Senate to thank the European Parliament. On 26 March, the European Parliament voted the Italian line on remigrazione, on repatriation, and on third-country structures, he said. He framed Italy as the architect of an emerging European deportation regime — and embraced a word that until that morning had belonged to neonazi street groups.

The word is not new. The French writer Renaud Camus coined it alongside his "Great Replacement" conspiracy theory — the claim that Western elites are deliberately swapping native populations for non-white migrants. Identitarians in France carried it into mainstream politics through the 2010s; Austrian activist Martin Sellner reframed it as a three-phase plan in his manifesto Remigration: A Proposal; in 2022 French presidential candidate Éric Zemmour campaigned on creating a Ministry of Remigration. By 2025 the Austrian FPÖ, Germany's AfD and other European far-right parties were using it openly; in the US, the Trump administration's Department of Homeland Security adopted the language in official communications.

The trace is striking. According to the Center on Strategic Online Hate's January 2026 report, the term went from 32,000 social-media mentions across the entire 2016–2022 window to 1.4 million in 2023–2025; the week of 1–8 September 2025 alone produced 71,000. Remigrazione was the German Unwort des Jahres for 2023 and the centre of Correctiv's January 2024 scoop on the AfD's Potsdam meeting. The path the report describes — a fringe slogan crossing into mainstream office through repetition, institutional validation, and algorithmic amplification — is the path Italy is now late-arriving on.

A scrape of every Camera stenografico from 2018 onwards plus every Senato resoconto d'aula returns 68 paragraphs containing the term, all between April 2024 and May 2026. The full XVIII Legislatura (2018–2022) — four years of Salvinian rhetoric — yields zero. The first Senate trace is a written question, filed in April 2024 by senators Unterberger and Spagnolli (Per le Autonomie) about Sellner's planned Italian visits. The first plenary utterance comes nine months later, on 8 January 2025, from Lega deputy Rossano Sasso, after New Year's Eve incidents: The time has come to act even more harshly.

Of those 68 mentions, 48 — over 70% — come from opposition benches (PD, M5S, AVS, Azione, +Europa), used to attack a government they say has already enacted what the word means. Only seven come from the governing coalition: five from Sasso, one from Lega deputy Domenico Furgiuele defending the cancelled CasaPound conference at Camera (Remigrazione e Riconquista, 30 January 2026), and one from Lisei. The continental pattern is reproduced exactly: extra-parliamentary groups launch the slogan, mainstream parties distance themselves from the word but enact what it implies — the Italy–Tunisia memorandum (July 2023), the Italy–Albania protocol (November 2023), and the EU asylum reform Lisei celebrated. The UK's 2022 Rwanda scheme drew sharp criticism from the Council of Europe's human-rights commissioner; three years later, the EU is normalising the same model.

Lisei's speech crosses the line CasaPound was kept from crossing. He spoke the word, claimed it for the governing party, and the chamber voted on. In Italy, the non-word has become a parliamentary one.

Data and methods

This piece relies on two primary sources — the stenographic records of the Camera dei Deputati and the Akoma Ntoso bulk export of the Senato della Repubblica — passed through a small R + shell pipeline before the timeline figure renders the curated moments. The walkthrough below is in build order.

1. Sources

  • Camera dei Deputati — plenary resoconti stenografici from the official portal www.camera.it, one HTML page per session at documenti.camera.it/leg{LL}/resoconti/assemblea/html/sed{NNNN}/stenografico.htm.
  • Senato della Repubblica — official Akoma Ntoso bulk repository SenatoDellaRepubblica/AkomaNtosoBulkData, one resaula/*-ra.akn.xml file per resoconto d'aula.
  • Camera deputy schede — biographical and group-history pages at documenti.camera.it/apps/commonServices/getDocumento.ashx?sezione=deputati&tipoDoc=schedaDeputato&idLegislatura=19&idPersona={id}, used to attribute each speech to the speaker's parliamentary group at the date of the speech.

2. Search pattern

A single case-insensitive regex — remigra[a-z]* — is applied to every paragraph. It matches all morphological forms (remigrazione, remigrare, remigrato, remigrazioni, …) without false positives in Italian. The pattern is defined once in code/00-setup.R and reused by every downstream step.

3. Camera scrape

A small shell pipeline (code/01-scrape-camera.sh, built on curl + xargs) probes every candidate session number for legislatures XVIII (full, 2018–2022) and XIX (full to the most recent session — 653 as of May 2026). Each fetched HTML is stream-grepped for /remigra/i and discarded if it doesn't match — only sessions containing the term remain on disk.

4. Camera extraction

For each retained Camera HTML hit (code/02-extract-camera.R), the parser walks <p class="intervento"> and <p class="interventoVirtuale"> elements and attributes every matching paragraph to its speaker. The speaker block carries an idPersona identifier, which is used downstream to look up the deputy's group history. Output: data/processed/camera_hits.csv, 54 paragraph-level rows.

5. Senato extraction

The Senato bulk repo is sparse-checked-out for legislatures XVIII and XIX (code/03-extract-senato.R). The parser walks each Akoma Ntoso XML document and applies two attribution rules:

  • Matches inside <an:speech> are attributed to the speaker named in the corresponding <an:from> (and to the (GROUP) tag preceding the first speech paragraph).
  • Matches in Allegato B (written questions and answers) are tagged allegato_b: the source XML carries no paragraph-level signer for these, so they enter the dataset unattributed at the speaker level.

Output: data/processed/senato_hits.csv, 14 rows (3 plenary speeches + 11 Allegato B paragraphs).

6. Party attribution at the time of the speech

For each Camera deputy (code/04-party-lookup.R) the script fetches the cached scheda page once and parses out the full sequence of group memberships, with start and end dates. The group active at the date of the speech is then selected. This matters in cases where a deputy switches group mid-legislature — for instance, Rossano Sasso, who left Lega for the Misto group on 6 February 2026: paragraphs before that date are tagged Lega, paragraphs after are tagged Misto. The output (data/processed/all_hits.csv, 68 rows) is the joined Camera + Senato dataset with party and government/opposition coalition flags.

7. Curation: eight moments from sixty-eight

The full machine-readable dataset is 68 paragraphs across 17 plenary sessions. The figure shows eight, picked for the trajectory they trace rather than for raw frequency: each one marks a first, a breakthrough, or the peak.

  • 30 Apr 2024 — first time the word enters any Senato document, in a written question on Martin Sellner.
  • 8 Jan 2025 — first Camera plenary utterance, by Lega deputy Sasso.
  • 4 Jun 2025 — first Senate plenary use, by AVS senator De Cristofaro, as attack.
  • 5 Nov 2025 — AVS deputy Grimaldi reports a right-wing colleague using the term inside the Aula.
  • 30 Jan 2026 — PD deputy Casu speaks on the day of the cancelled CasaPound conference.
  • 3 Feb 2026 — Lega deputy Furgiuele defends from the floor the conference he had hosted.
  • 1 Apr 2026 — first Fratelli d'Italia senator (Lisei) to embrace the term approvingly in plenary. The breakthrough.
  • 22 Apr 2026 — peak session of the dataset (29 paragraphs); Sasso, now Misto, scolds his old coalition.

8. Figure

The visualisation is a vertical quote timeline rather than a chart. Each dot is one moment the word entered the parliamentary record; vertical position is chronology, dot colour is party, and — crucially — the content of each card is the speech itself. A strip plot was tried and discarded: it could show when and who, but not what, and the what is the whole point.

9. Caveats

  • Scope: plenary records only — Camera resoconti stenografici and Senato resoconti d'aula. Committee transcripts are out of scope; spot-checking the Senato committee files (sommcomm) returns zero matches anyway. Earlier legislatures (XVII and before) are excluded: the term entered European far-right vocabulary only around 2014 and was not used in Italian parliamentary discourse before its appearance in this dataset (April 2024).
  • Allegato B paragraphs are not signed at the paragraph level in the Senato Akoma Ntoso source. Those hits are tagged allegato_b and labelled accordingly in the figure, rather than attributed to a guessed speaker.
  • Speakers who switched parliamentary group mid-legislature are attributed to the group active at the date of the speech.

10. Reproducibility

All code, data and the replication package live at github.com/data-journalism-26/data-bit-2-giorgio_2. The full pipeline is one orchestrator: Rscript code/run_all.R. Each step is idempotent — re-running re-uses cached HTML, the Senato sparse clone, and the deputy scheda pages.
